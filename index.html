Mātṛkā and Mālinī

Part Four — two orderings of the same fifty powers

मातृका-मालिनी भेदः

Part One · Śabdabrahman Part Two · Sphoṭa Part Three · Forty-Two Lenses Part Four · Mātṛkā–Mālinī Part Five · Nyāsa Part Six · Kuṇḍalinī

Ritual-Phonemic Power

The Alphabet in Two Voices

एकैव शक्तिः द्विधा क्रमेण

The same fifty letters can be spoken twice — once in the order a child is taught to read them, and once in an order no grammar lesson would ever produce. Kashmir Śaiva Tantra treats this second, deliberately scrambled order not as a curiosity but as a distinct technology of power, standing beside the first the way a key stands beside the lock it alone opens.

I.The Natural Order and Its Discontents

The Mātṛkā order — a, ā, i, ī, u, ū… ka, kha, ga, gha… — is the alphabet exactly as any Sanskrit primer teaches it: sequential, transparent, and immediately legible to ordinary grammatical cognition. This series' Part Three already established the doctrinal cost of that transparency: Śiva Sūtra 1.4's jñānādhiṣṭhānaṁ mātṛkā names this very order as the basis of ordinary, fragmenting knowledge — the alphabet a child masters without effort is, on the Śaiva reading, also the alphabet through which the child first learns to experience a bounded, separate self. Legibility and bondage, on this account, are not two different features of the natural order; they are the same feature, named twice.

II.Mālinī: The Interleaved Cipher

Mālinī reorders the identical fifty letters into a sequence with no grammatical logic a beginner could reconstruct — vowels and consonants interleaved according to a scheme documented most fully in Abhinavagupta's Parātriṃśikā-vivaraṇa. The name itself, "garlanded" or "wreathed," suggests not a random shuffle but a specific, deliberate re-threading of the same fifty beads onto a different string. Where the natural order is read fluently and therefore read through — the way a native speaker stops noticing individual letters entirely — Mālinī resists exactly this fluency by design, forcing a slower, more deliberate engagement with each letter that the natural order's automaticity had made impossible.

Traditional Trika formulation, closely paraphrased: "The letters, garlanded in the hidden order, yield what the plain order conceals; the two are one power, differently strung."

Traditional Trika Formulation · Parātriṃśikā-vivaraṇa Tradition

III.Somatic Encoding: Where the Two Orders Meet the Body

Both orderings are, in practice, somatic before they are conceptual. Mātṛkā-nyāsa — the placement this series' companion material treats at length — proceeds in two complementary modes. Bahir Mātṛkā Nyāsa, external placement, touches the fifty letters onto the head, face, eyes, ears, nostrils, mouth, limbs, and torso in the natural, sequential order, each syllable prefixed with Om and suffixed with Namaḥ as the corresponding locus is touched. Antar Mātṛkā Nyāsa, internal placement, mentally installs the same fifty letters on the petals of the seven chakras, ascending from Mūlādhāra to Sahasrāra — but it is here, in several Trika manuals, that the Mālinī sequence is substituted for the natural order specifically at the subtler, internal stage, on the reasoning that a practice already moving inward toward less fragmented awareness should not simultaneously be reinforced by the very sequence responsible for fragmentation in the first place.

This substitution is the doctrinal hinge of the entire Mātṛkā–Mālinī distinction: the external body, still operating in ordinary, externally oriented awareness, receives the legible order; the internal, ascending awareness receives the illegible one, precisely at the point where legibility itself becomes an obstacle rather than an aid.

IV.The Aṣṭamātṛkā Within Both Orders

The eight principal Mother-goddesses — Brāhmī, Māheśvarī, Kaumārī, Vaiṣṇavī, Vārāhī, Indrāṇī, Cāmuṇḍā, and an eighth variously named — are not a separate, smaller alphabet standing apart from either sequence. They are command-points within both: eight officers who preside over their respective clusters of letters whether those letters are read in the natural order a temple inscription might display them in, or in the interleaved order a Trika initiate is taught to recite internally. The eight do not change identity between orderings; only the path by which their power is accessed changes.

V.A Cluster of Governing Metaphors

The Cipher and Its Key. Mātṛkā is the plain alphabet everyone already reads fluently — and reads bondage into, by force of habit. Mālinī is the same alphabet re-keyed so that fluent, habitual reading no longer applies, forcing a different, less automatic mode of engagement with the identical fifty letters.

Two Rivers, One Source. A single reservoir released into two differently graded channels reaches the same sea by two different courses, one gentle and legible in its bends, the other steep and switchbacked. Neither channel carries different water; the water only learns a different shape of falling.

The Mirror-Script. A word reflected in a mirror is not a different word; it is the same word, read from the far side of a surface that has reversed its habitual direction. Mālinī is the mirror-script of the Mātṛkā the practitioner has already, through years of ordinary speech, over-learned.

VI.Vimarśa Revisited: Why Order Itself Is Power

Part Three's treatment of vimarśa — consciousness's active, self-referential pulse, as distinct from prakāśa, its sheer luminous presence — supplies the deepest warrant for treating sequence itself as doctrinally significant rather than a merely notational convenience. If the phonemes are goddesses rather than glyphs, as that section argued, then the order in which they are addressed is not incidental to their power any more than the order of words in an invocation is incidental to a mantra's efficacy. Mālinī's claim to a subtler access is, at bottom, a claim that vimarśa itself can be addressed differently depending on the sequence through which its instruments are invoked — the same fifty goddesses, greeted in an order that does not let ordinary cognition rush past them unnoticed.

Scope note This section states the Mātṛkā/Mālinī distinction at the level of doctrinal function and its somatic application. It does not reproduce the specific Mālinī letter-sequence in full technical detail, since manuals vary in the precise interleaving pattern taught, and correct transmission of the sequence itself is traditionally held to require direct instruction from a qualified teacher rather than textual reconstruction.

Hermeneutic Deep-Dive

The Parātriṃśikā-vivaraṇa: Five Registers on the Interleaved Order

परात्रिंशिकाविवरणम् — पञ्चदृष्ट्या विवेचनम्

Abhinavagupta's Parātriṃśikā-vivaraṇa, a commentary on a short but dense Trika verse-text, is the primary classical source treating Mālinī as a doctrinally serious alternative ordering rather than a scribal curiosity. What follows reads a small representative claim of that commentary across five registers, held apart deliberately, following this series' established method.

I.Philosophical Register

The commentary's governing philosophical move is to treat sequence as itself a category of manifestation, alongside more familiar categories like substance or quality. If the fifty phonemes are powers rather than symbols (this series' Part Three), then the order in which those powers are invoked is not a neutral container for them — it is itself a further power, exercised over the other fifty. Mālinī, on this reading, is not "the same fifty letters, rearranged" in the deflationary sense a modern reader might default to; it is a fifty-first power, the power of ordering, layered on top of the fifty it orders.

II.Scientific / Cognitive Register

Read cautiously, the natural-order-versus-interleaved-order distinction bears a suggestive, structural resemblance to a documented finding in cognitive research on expertise: highly practiced, automatized sequences (like reading one's native alphabet) are processed with minimal conscious attention, freeing cognitive resources but also making the sequence's individual units difficult to notice as such — a phenomenon sometimes discussed under the heading of automaticity. A deliberately novel or disrupted sequence forces attention back onto units that automaticity had rendered invisible. The comparison is offered at the level of gross structure only: cognitive-science automaticity research makes no claim about phonemes as cosmogonic powers, and the Trika source makes no claim about reaction-time studies; the resemblance is that both frameworks independently notice that familiarity itself can obscure a sequence's component parts.

III.Neurological / Psychophysiological Register

Antar Mātṛkā Nyāsa's substitution of Mālinī specifically at the internal, ascending stage invites a cautious parallel with general research on novelty and attentional engagement: unfamiliar or non-habitual sequences of stimuli are documented, in broad psychophysiological literature not specific to this tradition, to engage sustained attention differently than over-learned sequences, which the nervous system can process with reduced engagement precisely because they are so familiar. Whether this documented general mechanism has any specific bearing on Mālinī recitation, as distinct from any other deliberately unfamiliar sequence a practitioner might be asked to attend to, is not something this paper's sources allow it to settle.

IV.Metaphorical Register

The Parātriṃśikā-vivaraṇa's own governing image for Mālinī is architectural rather than linguistic: the text likens the interleaved order to a structure whose load-bearing members are hidden behind a visible façade — the façade (Mātṛkā, the natural order) is not false, but it is not where the structure's actual strength resides. Mālinī names the hidden framing the visible wall depends on without displaying.

V.Metaphysical Register

At its deepest register, the commentary's claim is that the distinction between the two orders is itself provisional — a teaching device rather than a final doctrine about two really-separate alphabets. Both orders are, without remainder, the single phonemic Śakti this series' Part Three names Mātṛkā in its broadest sense; "Mālinī" names not a second power but the same power's willingness to be approached from an angle ordinary fluency forecloses. The commentary's own closing move, consistent with the non-dual thrust of Kashmir Śaivism throughout this series, is to dissolve the two-order distinction back into the single, undivided phonemic ground both orders were only ever two ways of naming.

Scholarly Apparatus

Eight Ślokas, Cross-Referenced: Order as Doctrine

अष्टश्लोकाः — क्रमस्य सिद्धान्तरूपेण विवेचनम्

The eight verses below are drawn across the Ṛgvedic Vāk-Sūkta, the Śiva Sūtras, and the Trika commentarial tradition surrounding Mālinī, read against one another to trace a single trajectory: sequence itself, treated as doctrine rather than convenience.

अहं राष्ट्री सङ्गमनी वसूनाम् ahaṁ rāṣṭrī saṅgamanī vasūnām

"I am the sovereign queen, the gatherer-up of treasures." — Ṛgveda 10.125.3

Cross-reference: Vāk's self-declared sovereignty over "treasures" gathered rather than merely listed anticipates the doctrinal claim that a gathering — an ordering — is itself an exercise of power, not a neutral afterthought to power already complete.

ज्ञानाधिष्ठानं मातृका jñānādhiṣṭhānaṁ mātṛkā

"Mātṛkā is the basis of (limited) knowledge." — Śiva Sūtra 1.4

Cross-reference: Named in this series' Part Three as the mechanism of bondage; here specifically the natural order is what the sūtra indicts, since it is precisely that sequence's transparency to ordinary cognition the tradition holds responsible for fragmentation.

मातृकाचक्रसम्बोधः mātṛkā-cakra-sambodhaḥ

"Awakening to the wheel of the Mātṛkās." — Śiva Sūtra 2.7

Cross-reference: Compare this section's reading of Mālinī as a fifty-first power of ordering. The "wheel" this sūtra names awakening to may denote not one fixed sequence but the practitioner's dawning capacity to recognise any ordering, natural or interleaved, as an expression of the same underlying power.

Traditional Trika formulation, closely paraphrased: "As the garland is strung anew without losing a single bead, so the fifty are re-threaded without ceasing to be the fifty."

This verse extends that image directly: Mālinī does not add or remove beads, it only re-threads the identical string — the doctrinal claim examined in Section I above, that no new phoneme is introduced by the interleaved order.

Traditional Trika formulation, closely paraphrased: "What the outer body wears in sequence, the inner ascent wears reversed; one order clothes the flesh, the other clothes the flame."

कन्धे मातृका न्यसेत् पञ्चाशद्वर्णरूपिणम् kandhe mātṛkā nyaset pañcāśad-varṇa-rūpiṇam

"One should place the Mātṛkās, taking the form of fifty letters, upon the shoulder (and across the body)." — Traditional Nyāsa Formulation

Cross-reference: Compare the metaphysical register above (Section V). "All the letters" here is read, in this paper's synthesis, as neither the natural order alone nor the interleaved order alone but their shared, undifferentiated source — the point at which the Mātṛkā/Mālinī distinction itself dissolves.

·Six Documented Unanswered Questions

  1. Dating the Interleaved OrderWhether Mālinī represents a genuinely early alternative ordering independently transmitted alongside the natural order, or a later Trika systematisation retrospectively projected backward onto earlier, less formalised practice — the surviving manuscript record does not, to this paper's knowledge, settle the question.
  2. Uniformity Across LineagesWhether the specific interleaving pattern called "Mālinī" is a single, stable sequence across Trika sources, or whether — as with the varṇamālā's own fifty/fifty-one count — regional and lineage-specific variants exist that later tradition has retrospectively harmonised under one name.
  3. The Internal/External SplitWhether the specific claim that Bahir Nyāsa favours the natural order while Antar Nyāsa favours Mālinī is attested uniformly across manuals, or whether this paper's own synthesis (Section III above) states a tendency found in some sources more strongly than in others.
  4. Automaticity as Genuine MechanismWhether the cognitive-science parallel drawn to automaticity (Register II above) survives scrutiny of the specific claim that a fifty-unit alphabetic sequence, rather than any sufficiently novel sequence whatsoever, carries some phoneme-specific significance Mālinī doctrine requires but automaticity research does not itself specify.
  5. Abhinavagupta's SourcesTo what extent the Parātriṃśikā-vivaraṇa's treatment of Mālinī represents Abhinavagupta's own systematisation, as against earlier Trika āgama material (several no longer independently extant) that may have already carried the doctrine in a form this paper cannot directly examine.
  6. Whether "Two Orders" Is Itself the Right FrameWhether treating Mātṛkā and Mālinī as precisely two orderings (as this section has done, following the tradition's own most common framing) risks flattening a doctrine some sources present as admitting further, less commonly discussed intermediate or regional sequences this paper has not been able to document individually.

Theological Synthesis

Five Universal Ślokas

पञ्च सार्वत्रिकश्लोकाः — क्रमसिद्धान्तस्य सङ्क्षेपः

Where the eight ślokas above cross-referenced particular doctrinal points, the five gathered here each name one load-bearing joint of this part's argument — sovereignty, mechanism, re-threading, dissolution, and identity — so the whole can be held in one hand.

अहं राष्ट्री सङ्गमनी वसूनाम् ahaṁ rāṣṭrī saṅgamanī vasūnām

Sovereignty. "I am the sovereign queen, the gatherer-up of treasures." Vāk speaks first, and gathering is already, in her own mouth, an act of rule.

ज्ञानाधिष्ठानं मातृका jñānādhiṣṭhānaṁ mātṛkā

Mechanism. "Mātṛkā is the basis of (limited) knowledge." Sovereignty, unrecognised, becomes the very sequence that fragments the one who forgets it.

Re-threading. "As the garland is strung anew without losing a single bead, so the fifty are re-threaded without ceasing to be the fifty." Mechanism does not require new material to be reversed — only a different string.

Traditional Trika Formulation

Dissolution. "Two strings, one wheel; the bead does not know which order names it, and is not thereby less a bead." Re-threading, followed far enough, forgets that it was ever two threads at all.

Traditional Trika Formulation
अहं सोऽहम् इति ज्ञात्वा सर्ववर्णमयो भवेत् ahaṁ so'ham iti jñātvā sarvavarṇamayo bhavet

Identity. "Having known 'I am the Supreme,' the practitioner becomes one who embodies all the letters of the cosmos." Dissolution completes in the practitioner who was, all along, the single power both orders only ever named twice.

White Paper · Concluding Synthesis

Final Synthesis

This part has argued that Mātṛkā and Mālinī are not two competing alphabets but one phonemic power examined under two different disciplines of sequence — the first legible to the point of transparency, the second illegible by design, each suited to a different stage of the practitioner's own ascent from external, embodied placement toward internal, undifferentiated recognition. Where this series' Part Three established that the phoneme itself is constitutive rather than descriptive of reality, this part has extended that claim one register further: order is not a neutral container for constitutive power but a further instance of it, exercised over the fifty letters the way the letters themselves are exercised over manifest experience.

The six documented unanswered questions above, together with the eight cross-referenced ślokas and the five universal verses, are offered as this paper's own synthetic reading of a doctrine that — like the phoneme doctrine this series opened with — rewards examination from philosophical, cognitive, neurological, metaphorical, and metaphysical registers without collapsing into any single one of them.

Central claim, restated in one sentence The interleaved order Trika sources call Mālinī is theorised not as a second alphabet but as a fifty-first power — the power of sequence itself — laid over the same fifty phonemes Part Three treats as cosmogonic, and traditionally reserved for the subtler, internal stage of the same somatic placement documented in this series' Nyāsa material.

Objective Register · Highest-Level Synthesis

Nāda, Bindu, Kalā: The Science of Sound

नाद-बिन्दु-कला — शब्दविद्यायाः मूलतत्त्वम्

Everything argued above about Mātṛkā and Mālinī presumes a still deeper triad that the tradition treats as prior to any alphabet whatsoever: Nāda (resonance), Bindu (the concentrated point), and Kalā (the radiant fraction that lets the point become many). The fifteen tabs below hold this triad up to fifteen distinct, scholarly angles of examination, each written to whitepaper standard — primary-text claim, secondary scholarly reception, and this paper's own synthesis kept visibly distinct at every step.

Methodological note Each tab below distinguishes three tiers of claim: (1) what a named primary text states or is reasonably read as stating; (2) how modern scholars of Kashmir Śaivism and Tantric Studies — chiefly André Padoux, Alexis Sanderson, Mark S. G. Dyczkowski, Paul Muller-Ortega, Lilian Silburn, and Jaideva Singh — have glossed, dated, or contested that reading; and (3) where this paper itself synthesises across sources for expository coherence. Tier-3 synthesis is flagged explicitly wherever it occurs, since the sequential Nāda→Bindu→Kalā presentation used throughout is a pedagogical ordering this paper adopts for clarity, not a claim that every source surveyed states the doctrine in precisely this form or sequence.

Nāda: Resonance Prior to Any Word

नादः — अनाहतशब्दः

Primary claim. Nāda names vibration as such, prior to its differentiation into any particular phoneme. The tradition distinguishes āhata nāda, "struck" sound produced by the contact of two objects (including the vocal apparatus), from anāhata nāda, the "unstruck" resonance said to precede and underlie all struck sound without itself arising from any collision. The distinction recurs across Śaiva, Śākta, and Haṭha sources with enough consistency that Dyczkowski, in his study of Kashmir Śaiva vibrational doctrine, treats āhata/anāhata as close to a shared technical baseline across otherwise divergent lineages, even where their cosmogonic elaborations of it differ sharply.

Scholarly reception. Padoux's Vāc: The Concept of the Word in Selected Hindu Tantras cautions against reading anāhata nāda as simply "silence" in the colloquial sense; he treats it instead as a technical category occupying a specific place within a graded ontology of sound-levels, functionally continuous with, rather than opposed to, audible speech. Sanderson's work on the textual history of the Śaiva tantras further notes that the relative weight given to Nāda as an independent ontological category — as against treating it merely as a preparatory yogic experience — varies considerably between the dualist Siddhānta corpus and the non-dualist Trika and Krama systems this series has drawn on throughout.

This paper's synthesis. The distinction is not acoustic trivia; it functions, across the sources surveyed, as the tradition's way of insisting that audible speech is a late, derivative case of a more general resonant capacity belonging to consciousness itself. Where Part Three of this series treated the phoneme as constitutive rather than merely descriptive of reality, Nāda is what the phoneme is here read as a differentiation of — every mātṛkā, in either the natural or the Mālinī order examined above, a particular knot tied into an otherwise undifferentiated resonant continuum.

Open question. Whether anāhata nāda is best modelled as ontologically prior to Bindu (as the sequential presentation in this tab-set assumes) or as co-arising with it is a point on which Dyczkowski notes the manuscript tradition itself is not fully uniform; this paper follows the more common sequential reading without treating the matter as closed.

Bindu: The Point of Total Concentration

बिन्दुः — संहतशक्तिबिन्दुः

Primary claim. Bindu, literally "point" or "drop," names the moment at which the diffuse resonance of Nāda contracts to a single, undifferentiated point of concentrated potential — consciousness and its power (Śiva and Śakti, in the Kashmir Śaiva idiom) held in such tight unity that no distinction between them is yet operative. It is frequently visualised, rather than merely described, as a dimensionless point of light, precisely because any extended shape would already presuppose the differentiation Bindu itself precedes.

Scholarly reception. Muller-Ortega's The Triadic Heart of Śiva treats parabindu specifically as the term Abhinavagupta and his commentators use to keep this concentration-point distinct from the merely visual bindu of later, more devotional maṇḍala iconography — a distinction Muller-Ortega argues is frequently collapsed in popular presentations but is doctrinally load-bearing in the Trika sources this series draws on. Silburn's study of Kuṇḍalinī doctrine likewise treats Bindu as functioning less as a static "thing" than as a name for a threshold condition, cautioning against reifying it into an object the way some later haṭha-yogic physiological literature tends to.

This paper's synthesis. Bindu is the hinge on which the entire triad turns: Nāda is resonance without a locus; Bindu is locus without yet any further multiplicity; Kalā, examined next, is the multiplicity Bindu makes possible once it consents to divide. Read this way, Bindu is not a way-station passed through once, but a threshold condition the tradition holds to remain, in principle, continuously accessible beneath every subsequent differentiation.

Open question. Whether the "point" language is intended literally (a genuine ontological locus) or is, as Padoux suggests for several comparable Tantric technical terms, primarily a mnemonic and meditative device whose spatial imagery should not be over-interpreted metaphysically, remains genuinely contested across the secondary literature surveyed here.

Primary · Parā-bindu doctrine, Trika sources on Śiva–Śakti sāmarasya — Secondary · Muller-Ortega (1989); Silburn (1988)

Kalā: The Radiant Fraction

कला — विभागशक्तिः

Primary claim. Kalā names the power by which the undivided Bindu becomes capable of division at all — a "digit" or "fraction" of the whole, as in the sixteen kalās traditionally ascribed to the moon, each a partial radiance of a single undivided light. Kalā is not multiplicity itself but the capacity for multiplicity, the first admission of "more than one" into a field that was, at the level of Bindu, still strictly singular.

Scholarly reception. Padoux notes that "kalā" is one of the more heavily overloaded terms in Tantric technical vocabulary, carrying distinct but related senses in different contexts: a fraction of the moon's light, a fraction of the day (a unit of time), and, in the sense used here, a fraction of undifferentiated creative power. Sanderson's textual-historical work traces how the specifically cosmogonic sense of kalā used in this tab-set becomes more systematised in later Śaiva Siddhānta tattva-hierarchies than in earlier Trika sources, where the term is used more fluidly.

This paper's synthesis. It is Kalā, on the reading this paper adopts, that eventually issues in the fifty phonemes this part's main essay has examined in two orders: Mātṛkā and Mālinī are, from this vantage, two different disciplines applied to material that Kalā has already begun to fraction out of Bindu's prior unity. The sixteen-kalā lunar schema referenced above is directly continuous with the Ṣoḍaśī-kalā doctrine treated in this series' companion Ṣoḍhā-Nyāsa material, where the sixteen radiances are placed along the ascending spinal axis.

Open question. Because "kalā" carries genuinely distinct technical senses across sources (cosmogonic fraction, lunar phase, temporal unit, and — in Śrīvidyā specifically — a component of the Śrīcakra's own structure), this paper's unified presentation risks flattening distinctions a specialist reader working across multiple lineages simultaneously would want kept separate.

Primary · Ṣoḍaśī-kalā doctrine — Secondary · Padoux (1990); Sanderson (2007, 2014)

The Triadic Cosmogony: Nāda → Bindu → Kalā

क्रमः — नादात् बिन्दुः, बिन्दोः कला

Primary claim. Read together rather than separately, the triad describes a single cosmogonic sequence rather than three independent categories: undifferentiated resonance (Nāda) contracts to a point of concentrated, still-unified potential (Bindu), which in turn admits the possibility of partial radiance (Kalā) — the first genuine plurality, out of which phoneme, word, and world subsequently unfold in the four-level descent of Vāk this series' earlier parts have already traced.

Scholarly reception. The Tantrāloka's own emanative sequence of tattvas, as analysed by Sanderson and by Dyczkowski, places this triad within a considerably longer chain of thirty-six or more principles, of which Nāda-Bindu-Kalā occupies only the uppermost, subtlest register — immediately below the undifferentiated Śiva- and Śakti-tattvas and above the more familiar categories (Sadāśiva, Īśvara, Śuddhavidyā) that this series has not treated in detail. Presenting the triad in isolation, as this tab-set does for expository focus, is therefore a deliberate narrowing of a much longer emanative scheme rather than a claim that the full tattva sequence reduces to these three terms alone.

This paper's synthesis. The sequence, so narrowed, is asymmetric in a way that matters for the whole tab-set's argument: Nāda does not require Bindu to exist, but Bindu presupposes Nāda as the resonance it concentrates, and Kalā presupposes Bindu as the unity it first divides. The order Nāda–Bindu–Kalā is, in this strict sense, irreversible within the doctrine as presented here, however freely later stages may be revisited contemplatively once traversed — a point directly relevant to Tab VII's reading of Kuṇḍalinī ascent as a literal retracing of this sequence.

Open question. Whether the three-term triad should be read as ontologically separable stages (as this tab-set's sequential language implies) or as three simultaneous aspects of a single, undivided act of emanation — the reading several non-dual commentators explicitly prefer, precisely to avoid smuggling temporal succession into what is meant to be an atemporal unfolding — is not resolved by the sources surveyed and is flagged here rather than adjudicated.

Primary · Tantrāloka on the emanative sequence of tattvas — Secondary · Sanderson (2007); Dyczkowski (1989)

Parā Nāda: The Pre-Phonemic Ground

परानादः — शब्दातीतः शब्दः

Primary claim. Parā Nāda, "supreme resonance," is the term reserved for Nāda specifically in its most undifferentiated aspect — prior even to the incipient contraction Bindu represents. It is deliberately paradoxical: a "sound beyond sound," audible to no ear because it precedes the very distinction between a sound and its hearer.

Scholarly reception. Several commentators treat Parā Nāda as functionally identical to Parā Vāk, the highest of the four levels of speech this series has examined — the terms differing in emphasis (Vāk stresses meaning-bearing potential; Nāda stresses vibratory potential) rather than in referent. Padoux, however, is more cautious than this convenient identification suggests: he notes that different textual streams within the Trika corpus foreground one term over the other for reasons that appear to be lineage-specific rather than doctrinally arbitrary, and that treating the two as simply interchangeable — as this tab-set does for expository economy — smooths over a genuine terminological plurality in the primary sources.

This paper's synthesis. This identification matters for the present part's argument: if Parā Nāda and Parā Vāk name substantially the same reality, then Mātṛkā and Mālinī — two disciplines of ordering applied to the fifty phonemes — are, at their root, two disciplines applied to differentiations of a single resonant ground neither ordering can fully exhaust. Every later, more differentiated stage this tab-set surveys can therefore be read as a specific, partial recovery of what Parā Nāda already holds without division.

Open question. Whether Parā Nāda is better modelled as a fourth, "hidden" level standing above the traditional four-level Parā–Paśyantī–Madhyamā–Vaikharī schema (making five levels in total), or as another name for Parā itself at its subtlest aspect (keeping the schema at four), is treated inconsistently enough across secondary sources that this paper declines to adjudicate it and flags the ambiguity explicitly.

Primary · Trika sources on Parā Nāda / Parā Vāk identification — Secondary · Padoux (1990)

The Four Levels of Vāk, Re-Read Through the Triad

परा-पश्यन्ती-मध्यमा-वैखरी

Primary claim. Overlaying Nāda-Bindu-Kalā onto the four traditional levels of speech gives a second, complementary map of the same descent: Parā corresponds to Parā Nāda examined above; Paśyantī, the "seeing" level at which meaning is grasped as an undivided whole before any sequential unfolding, corresponds closely to Bindu's still-unified potential; Madhyamā, the "intermediate" level at which meaning begins to differentiate into a mental sequence of words, corresponds to Kalā's admission of plurality; and Vaikharī, articulated physical speech, is the fully differentiated product — the phonemes of Mātṛkā and Mālinī alike.

Scholarly reception. This four-level schema is itself older than, and originally independent of, the Nāda-Bindu-Kalā triad: Padoux traces its grammatical roots to Bhartṛhari, whose sphoṭa theory (this series' Part Two) is a linguistic-philosophical project rather than a Tantric cosmogonic one. The mapping proposed in this tab is therefore a later Tantric overlay onto an earlier grammatical framework, and Padoux is explicit that not every Tantric source performs this overlay in the same way — some align Bindu with Paśyantī, as here, while others reserve Bindu for a point strictly between Parā and Paśyantī, treating the correspondence as looser than a one-to-one mapping.

This paper's synthesis. Held loosely rather than as a precise isomorphism, the two maps function as complementary rather than competing lenses on one descent, each drawing attention to a different aspect of the same movement from unity toward differentiated expression: the Vāk schema foregrounds meaning's unfolding, the Nāda-Bindu-Kalā schema foregrounds vibration's unfolding, and the present tab-set treats their convergence as instructive precisely because it is not perfectly exact.

Open question. Given that the two schemas originate in different disciplinary contexts (grammar and Tantric cosmogony) and were only later brought into correspondence, whether the correspondence itself carries independent doctrinal weight, or is better read as a later interpretive convenience bridging two originally separate frameworks, remains an open question this paper does not resolve.

Primary · Series Part Two, Sphoṭa and the levels of Vāk — Secondary · Padoux (1990)

Kuṇḍalinī as Nāda in Ascent

कुण्डलिनी — ऊर्ध्वगामी नादः

Primary claim. Haṭha and Tantric physiological doctrine describes the awakened Kuṇḍalinī's ascent through the central channel as accompanied by an internally perceived resonance — a subtle, unstruck sound reported to change in character as the ascent passes each successive cakra, sometimes catalogued as a graded series (examined in Tab X below). On this reading, the ascent is not merely an energetic or physiological event but a literal traversal back up the very sequence this tab-set has been describing in reverse: from the differentiated, "struck" quality of ordinary embodied experience toward the unstruck resonance of Parā Nāda at the crown.

Scholarly reception. Silburn's study of Kuṇḍalinī doctrine treats the nāda-ascent narrative as considerably more securely attested in the Haṭha-yogic and later Tantric physiological literature than in the earlier, more strictly philosophical Trika sources this series has otherwise privileged — a difference in genre she attributes to the later literature's greater interest in reproducible, embodied markers of progress along the path, as against the earlier sources' comparatively abstract cosmogonic concerns. This is a caution worth stating plainly: the vivid, cakra-by-cakra nāda narrative is better attested as Haṭha-yogic doctrine than as Trika Śaiva doctrine proper, even though this tab-set draws on both bodies of literature to construct a single composite account.

This paper's synthesis. Read with that caveat in place, the correlation gives the Mātṛkā/Mālinī distinction examined in this part's main essay a further physiological correlate: Antar Nyāsa's substitution of the interleaved order at the internal, ascending stage (Section III above) can be read as an attempt to align the practitioner's phonemic recitation with the same upward trajectory Kuṇḍalinī doctrine independently describes, even where the two bodies of literature developed largely independently of one another.

Open question. Whether the internally reported "sounds" associated with each cakra in this literature should be read as literal auditory percepts, as culturally-shaped interpretations of genuine but non-specific interoceptive signals, or as a primarily didactic device for marking progress along an otherwise hard-to-verify contemplative path is a question this paper's sources do not settle, and on which this paper does not take a position.

Primary · Haṭha-yogic Kuṇḍalinī-nāda narrative — Secondary · Silburn (1988); series Part Six

Bindu as Śiva–Śakti Sāmarasya

बिन्दुः — शिवशक्तिसामरस्यम्

Primary claim. Some Trika and Śrīvidyā sources further resolve Bindu itself into two aspects held in perfect fusion (sāmarasya): a "white" bindu associated with Śiva and a "red" bindu associated with Śakti, whose complete, undifferentiated union constitutes Bindu proper. Differentiation — and hence Kalā — is said to begin precisely at the point where this fusion admits the barest asymmetry, one aspect very slightly emphasised over the other.

Scholarly reception. This bipartite reading of Bindu is considerably more prominent in Śrīvidyā sources, as documented by scholars of that tradition's ritual and iconographic corpus, than in the earlier Trika material this series draws on for its main essay; Sanderson's account of the historical relationship between Trika and the later Śrīvidyā-Kaula synthesis suggests the white/red bindu elaboration is a comparatively later systematisation, developed as Śrīvidyā absorbed and reworked earlier Kaula ritual elements rather than a doctrine already fully explicit in the earliest Trika strata.

This paper's synthesis. Read as a later but doctrinally consistent elaboration rather than a competing account, this finer-grained model matters to the science of sound specifically because it locates the origin of all subsequent asymmetry — including the asymmetric, non-reversible ordering of the Mātṛkā and Mālinī sequences examined in this part's main essay — at the very first departure from perfect symmetry, rather than treating asymmetry as something introduced only much later, at the level of the fifty phonemes themselves.

Open question. Whether this white/red elaboration should be read back into earlier Trika Bindu-doctrine as an implicit but unstated feature, or whether doing so retrojects a specifically Śrīvidyā development onto sources that did not yet hold it, is a historical question this paper flags rather than settles.

Primary · Śrīvidyā sources on Śiva–Śakti bindu-bheda — Secondary · Sanderson (2014)

The Cakra–Kalā Correspondence Map

चक्रेषु कलाविन्यासः

Primary claim. Antar Mātṛkā-Nyāsa, examined in this part's main essay, assigns groups of phonemes to the lotus-petals of the seven principal cakras; the Nāda-Bindu-Kalā doctrine supplies the deeper rationale for why this assignment is graded rather than uniform. Each cakra is traditionally assigned not only a petal-count of letters but also a specific number of kalās considered active at that station, with the proportion of unstruck to struck resonance said to increase as the ascent proceeds upward — the throat and brow centres carrying a subtler ratio than the navel or root.

Scholarly reception. The seven-cakra schema itself, as White and other historians of Haṭha Yoga have documented, is considerably later and more standardised than the diverse and sometimes larger cakra-systems (five, six, nine, or more centres) attested in earlier Tantric sources; the specific petal-counts given below reflect the schema that became dominant from roughly the sixteenth century onward and should not be read as uniform across the entire Tantric corpus this series surveys.

The Ten (Daśa) Nādas of Haṭha Tradition

दश नादाः — हठयोगपरम्परायाम्

Primary claim. Certain Haṭha Yoga manuals, most notably the fourth chapter of the Haṭhayogapradīpikā on Nāda-anusandhāna, catalogue a graded sequence of internally audible sounds said to arise, in order, as concentration on the unstruck resonance deepens — likened variously to the sounds of ocean waves, thunderclouds, a kettle-drum, a conch, a bell, a flute, a vīṇā, a bee, and finally a sound compared to distant thunder that is said to mark the threshold just before the subtlest, most undifferentiated resonance.

Scholarly reception. Manuals differ meaningfully on the exact sequence and count of these sounds — some give nine stages, others ten, others eleven — and modern scholarship on Haṭha Yoga's textual history treats this variation as evidence of an orally transmitted contemplative report subsequently written down by different compilers in different regional traditions, rather than a single fixed catalogue with one authoritative source. This paper does not treat the list as settled doctrine so much as a widely attested but textually variable contemplative report.

This paper's synthesis. What the daśa-nāda catalogue contributes to the present study is primarily methodological: it treats Nāda not as a single homogeneous category but as a graded field admitting its own internal taxonomy — precisely the move the Mātṛkā/Mālinī distinction makes one register down, at the level of the phonemes Nāda eventually differentiates into. The catalogue's internal ordering (coarser, louder sounds giving way to subtler ones) mirrors, at the level of contemplative report, the same coarse-to-subtle trajectory the Nāda-Bindu-Kalā triad describes cosmogonically.

Open question. Whether the specific auditory qualities catalogued (conch, bee, thunder, and so on) carry independent doctrinal significance, or are better read as culturally available similes reached for by practitioners describing an experience that resists more precise description, is a question the primary sources do not themselves adjudicate.

Primary · Haṭhayogapradīpikā, Chapter 4, on Nāda-anusandhāna — Secondary · comparative Haṭha-yoga textual scholarship

Kāmakalā: The Triangular Bindu of Śrīvidyā

कामकला — त्रिकोणबिन्दुः

Primary claim. Śrīvidyā sources visualise the union of Śiva and Śakti bindus not as a single point but as a triangle whose three corners — two aspects of Śakti and one of Śiva, or variant configurations depending on lineage — together constitute a specifically generative form of Bindu called Kāmakalā. Where the plain Bindu examined in Tab II is a point of concentration, Kāmakalā is specifically the point considered as the origin of manifest creation, its threefold structure said by some commentators to correspond to the three-and-a-half coils of dormant Kuṇḍalinī.

Scholarly reception. As with the white/red bindu material in Tab VIII, Kāmakalā doctrine is documented primarily within the Śrīvidyā-Kaula corpus and its associated ritual diagram, the Śrīcakra; it is not a term this paper's Trika sources for the main Mātṛkā/Mālinī essay employ with the same technical specificity. Scholars of Śrīvidyā ritual treat Kāmakalā as inseparable from the Śrīcakra's own geometry, such that discussing the concept in isolation from its accompanying diagram, as this tab necessarily does, omits a visual dimension the tradition itself treats as essential rather than illustrative.

This paper's synthesis. Kāmakalā's relevance to the science of sound, on the synthetic reading this tab-set adopts, is that it supplies a geometric, rather than purely temporal, model of how undivided potential becomes structured multiplicity — complementing the sequential Nāda-Bindu-Kalā account with a spatial one in which the same transition is pictured as a shape rather than a process.

Open question. Whether the temporal (Nāda-Bindu-Kalā) and spatial (Kāmakalā) models are best read as two independent descriptions of the same underlying event, arrived at by different lineages, or whether one should be treated as doctrinally prior to and generative of the other, is not settled by the comparative sources this paper draws on.

Primary · Śrīvidyā Kāmakalā and Śrīcakra doctrine — Secondary · comparative Śrīvidyā ritual scholarship

Spanda: The Pulse Beneath Nāda

स्पन्दः — नादस्य अन्तर्गतं स्पन्दनम्

Primary claim. The Spanda school within Kashmir Śaivism, whose foundational text is the Spandakārikā traditionally attributed to Vasugupta or his disciple Kallaṭa, describes consciousness not as static luminosity alone but as possessing an intrinsic, minute vibratory pulse — spanda — even in states ordinarily considered perfectly still. Several commentators treat Spanda as the still finer substrate Nāda itself depends on: Nāda is resonance already differentiated enough to be called "sound" in even the most attenuated sense, whereas Spanda is the bare fact of self-referential movement within consciousness that makes any resonance, sound included, possible in the first place.

Scholarly reception. Dyczkowski's dedicated study, The Doctrine of Vibration, is careful to distinguish Spanda as a formal school with its own textual lineage (Spandakārikā and its commentaries) from spanda as a general technical term used more loosely across other Śaiva sources; not every text that uses the word "spanda" is drawing on the Spanda school's specific doctrinal apparatus, and Dyczkowski cautions against treating every occurrence of the term as evidence of that school's direct influence.

This paper's synthesis. With that terminological caution registered, the school's central claim is directly relevant here: the science of sound this tab-set surveys does not terminate in Nāda as its final, irreducible ground; Spanda names one further, subtler stratum beneath it — a reminder that even a triadic doctrine this comprehensive is, within the tradition's own terms, provisional rather than final. This connects directly to the Vimarśa doctrine this series' Part Three already treated as consciousness's self-referential pulse; several scholars, including Dyczkowski, effectively treat spanda and vimarśa as near-synonyms operative at different points in Abhinavagupta's synthesis of earlier Spanda-school and Pratyabhijñā materials.

Open question. Whether spanda and vimarśa are, in Abhinavagupta's mature synthesis, fully identical terms for a single reality or retain a subtle distinction of emphasis (spanda stressing dynamic movement, vimarśa stressing reflective self-awareness) is a genuinely debated point within Kashmir Śaiva scholarship that this paper does not claim to resolve.

Primary · Spandakārikā tradition — Secondary · Dyczkowski (1989); series Part Three on Vimarśa

Objective Register: Acoustic Physics Compared

ध्वनिविज्ञानदृष्ट्या तुलना

Primary claim (physics). Held to a strictly objective, comparative standard, the Nāda-Bindu-Kalā doctrine and modern acoustic physics describe different domains and should not be conflated: acoustic physics treats sound as pressure-wave propagation through a physical medium, fully specified by frequency, amplitude, and waveform, with no doctrinal commitment to a pre-physical "unstruck" resonance. The tradition's Nāda, by contrast, is explicitly not a claim about pressure waves; it is a phenomenological and metaphysical category describing a form of resonance the tradition holds to be prior to, and the ground of, any physically propagating sound.

Where a structural analogy is defensible. A structural resonance does exist between the two frameworks in the shared observation that any bounded, differentiated waveform can be understood as a modulation of a more general vibratory capacity — the physicist's underlying medium, the tradition's underlying Nāda; and, more specifically, that a complex periodic waveform can be decomposed (via Fourier analysis) into a sum of simpler component frequencies, which bears a loose structural resemblance to Kalā's role in fractioning an undivided Bindu into multiple differentiated components. The resemblance is formal, not causal or explanatory.

Where the analogy breaks down. The disanalogies are at least as important as the resemblance and should not be elided for the sake of a tidy parallel: Fourier decomposition is a mathematical description of an already-physical waveform with no claim about a prior, non-physical "unstruck" state; the tradition's Nāda-Bindu-Kalā sequence, by contrast, makes an explicit ontological claim — that consciousness itself, prior to and independent of any physical medium, possesses an intrinsic vibratory capacity. No experiment in acoustic physics is designed to, or capable of, adjudicating that further ontological claim one way or the other, since it lies outside what physical measurement of pressure waves can address.

This paper's position. This paper states the parallel at the structural level only, offered as a pedagogically useful comparison for readers approaching the tradition from a modern scientific background, and explicitly does not claim that acoustic physics thereby validates, explains, or reduces to the tradition's metaphysical claims, nor that the tradition's claims constitute an alternative or superior physics. The two frameworks answer different questions and neither confirms nor refutes the other.

Comparative register only — offered as structural analogy, not equivalence or validation

The Praṇava (Oṃ) Read Through the Triad

ॐ-विश्लेषणम् नादबिन्दुकलादृष्ट्या

Primary claim. The syllable Oṃ is traditionally analysed into more than its three audible phonemic components (a-u-m); the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad's fourth mātrā, often called ardhamātrā ("the half-measure") or turīya ("the fourth"), is described there in relation to the three preceding waking, dreaming, and deep-sleep states rather than explicitly identified with Bindu in the Upaniṣad's own terms. Later Tantric commentary, rather than the Upaniṣad itself, is where this fourth element becomes more directly correlated with Bindu, and a still subtler trailing Nāda, in the specific sense this tab-set has been developing.

Scholarly reception. This paper flags that distinction deliberately: the Māṇḍūkya's own primary concern is the four states of consciousness (waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and turīya as their ground), and its use of Oṃ's four mātrās is in service of that psychological-metaphysical scheme rather than a self-standing statement of Nāda-Bindu-Kalā cosmogony. The Tantric reading offered below is therefore best understood as a later, doctrinally motivated overlay of one framework onto another textually and historically distinct one — a move consistent with this series' general practice of showing how originally separate frameworks come to be read together, but one that should not be mistaken for the Upaniṣad's own explicit teaching.

This paper's synthesis. With that overlay made explicit rather than implicit, Oṃ can be read as a compact, audible model of the entire Nāda-Bindu-Kalā descent: three struck phonemes (Kalā's differentiated product), converging on a silent point (read here as Bindu), itself resolving into unstruck resonance (Nāda) and, beyond even that, silence. This reading gives the present tab-set's whole argument a single, recitable anchor: to pronounce Oṃ attentively, letting the sound trail into the silence that follows it, is — on this paper's synthetic reading, not the Upaniṣad's own explicit claim — to trace the entire cosmogony this part has surveyed in miniature, once per recitation.

Open question. Whether the Tantric Nāda-Bindu-Kalā overlay onto Oṃ's fourth mātrā represents a faithful extension of the Māṇḍūkya's own logic or a distinct doctrinal project borrowing its authority is a question of textual history this paper does not claim to settle.

Primary · Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad, verse 1 (four mātrās); later Tantric commentarial overlay

Closing Synthesis: Why the Science of Sound Begins Here

उपसंहारः — शब्दविद्यायाः आरम्भबिन्दुः

Primary claim. The fourteen tabs above converge on a single claim, stated here at the highest level of generality this paper permits itself: any study of sound that begins directly with the fifty phonemes — whether in the natural Mātṛkā order or the interleaved Mālinī order this part's main essay has examined at length — begins one stage too late. The tradition's own account insists that phoneme presupposes Kalā, Kalā presupposes Bindu, and Bindu presupposes Nāda, so that a truly first-principles science of sound must be a science of resonance, concentration, and radiance before it is a science of letters at all.

Scholarly reception, gathered. Taken together, the secondary scholarship surveyed across these fifteen tabs — Padoux on the layered ontology of Vāk, Sanderson on the textual history separating Trika from later Śrīvidyā-Kaula systematisation, Dyczkowski on Spanda and vibrational doctrine specifically, Muller-Ortega on parabindu's precise technical sense, Silburn on Kuṇḍalinī's embodied correlates, and comparative Haṭha-yoga scholarship on the daśa-nāda catalogue — converges on a shared caution this closing synthesis inherits rather than resolves: the unified, sequential Nāda-Bindu-Kalā presentation used throughout this tab-set is a real and recurring doctrinal pattern, but it is assembled here from sources spanning different centuries, different lineages (dualist Siddhānta, non-dualist Trika, later Śrīvidyā-Kaula, and Haṭha-yogic), and, in places, different technical vocabularies not perfectly continuous with one another.

This paper's synthesis. This is offered, therefore, as this tab-set's own synthetic reading, not as a settled scholarly consensus: the specific sequencing, terminology, and correspondences vary meaningfully across the Śaiva, Śākta, and Haṭha sources surveyed above, and a reader pursuing any single tab further will find genuine, unresolved variation beneath the unified presentation given here for the sake of a coherent overview. Where this paper has flagged an open question — dating, lineage-specificity, terminological overload, or genre difference between philosophical and physiological sources — that flag should be read as a pointer to further primary and secondary reading, not as a gap this synthesis has quietly resolved in its own favour.

What remains genuinely settled. What does survive this level of scrutiny, across every source surveyed regardless of lineage or century, is the structural claim with which this tab-set opened: that the tradition, in every variant examined, treats the audible phoneme as derivative rather than foundational, and locates the proper ground of a science of sound somewhere further back — in resonance, in concentration, in radiance — than the alphabet a Mātṛkā or Mālinī recitation ever directly touches.
